Both policy makers and researchers are finding that it takes more than just building a grocery store and stocking it with healthy food.

What you as an individual can do to help:

  • If you recognize that you are living in a food desert, you can start by helping those in your community understand what this means and talk about ways to make change.
  • Discussing different options, such as growing your own food, working with local retailers to sell healthy, vegan foods, etc. is a good place to start.
  • It is important to bring your ideas and concerns to policy makers—city councilmembers, state legislators, etc.
  • Create petitions and have members of the community sign them to show support.

What community leaders and businesses can do to help?

Community leaders and businesses need to team up to combat this problem. Grocery stores in low-income communities can’t markup the price of their goods because of the clientele they are serving. To continue to turn a profit, grocery stores need to expand their range of offerings and market the store as more of a community center.

Some key tips for a thriving grocery store in a food desert

Changing the diet of individuals doesn’t happen overnight. Instead of trying to do a sweeping change in area, consider making small changes

  1. Take extra time to hand-stack fruits and veggies into little pyramids — because it avoids bruising and it's eye-catching.
  2. Invest in skilled butchers, fishmongers and in-store chefs to prepare healthier food options that customers can smell inside the store (like fire-grilled chicken instead of fried chicken).
  3. Conduct research with community leaders to learn about their backgrounds and to offer selections that will appeal to the community your store (e.g. stores in communities with a larger Muslim population have a separate department for Halal meat.)
  4. Offer ingredients/items that your community is interested in, but are hard to find, in a hot-food section of your store.
  5. Substitute some ingredients in recipes for healthier alternatives (e.g. collard greens cooked with smoked turkey instead of pork, as a healthy touch)
